Subculture once gave fashion trends their staying power. Does that still hold in the age of the algorithm? Five years of search data across eight keywords answers the question — and the findings are more complicated than expected.

Fashion has always moved in cycles — but for most of the twentieth century, those cycles were measured in years, not weeks. A trend earned cultural traction slowly, through music scenes and street communities, through the gradual process of an identity becoming visible. Athleisure didn't emerge from a single viral moment. It grew because a generation changed how it thought about the body, about work, about what dressing well actually meant.

That pipeline is now effectively broken. In January 2024, the mob wife aesthetic went from a single TikTok video to international press coverage in under a week. By March, it was over. The creators had moved on, the retailers had just started producing, and the algorithm had already served the next thing.

No two trends in this dataset are further apart. Athleisure has been growing for over a decade with no sign of slowing. Mob wife peaked in a single week and was functionally over within a month. The gap between them — in lifecycle length, average sustained interest, and speed of collapse — is the research question made visible. They were chosen because both are immediately recognizable, and because the contrast between them is not an edge case. It is the central finding.

Subculture once gave fashion trends their staying power. Does that still hold?

Using five years of weekly Google Trends search data across eight fashion keywords — collected via the Google Trends Python API as part of a graduate data analytics research project — this report attempts to quantify what the industry has long sensed but rarely measured. As Dick Hebdige argued in Subculture: The Meaning of Style (1979), subcultural fashion is not decoration but communication — and because it was rooted in identity rather than novelty, it had staying power. This project tests whether that claim still holds.

Slope Chart
Historical average vs. recent six months

Which trends is the algorithm still serving?

Not all trends that peaked are dead. Quiet luxury was declared over in January 2024 — but its recent six-month average is 53.9, higher than at any point in 2022. Tomato girl summer, by contrast, has a recent average of 1.1. Outside a single week in June 2023, it barely registered. The algorithm moved on before the summer ended.

Key finding
Balletcore has the highest runway validation of any viral trend in the dataset — and the steepest post-peak collapse at 95 points. Athleisure and slow fashion show the smallest drops, though both are still rising as of May 2026 and have not genuinely peaked within this dataset window. The pattern across viral trends is consistent: runway co-signs can extend a trend's lifespan but do not protect it from algorithmic abandonment.

For trend intelligence: post-peak drop rate is a computable diagnostic — available from any public search dataset — that gives a more precise read on algorithmic staying power than press coverage or runway mentions alone.

05
Reflection

What this data shows — and what it doesn't.

The gap between subculture-born and viral-born fashion trends is real and measurable in this dataset. Athleisure and slow fashion are still growing after five years. Tomato girl summer was relevant for approximately one week. The difference in average sustained interest between these two groups is not close.

The more complicated finding is at the edges. Cottagecore and dark academia — both rooted in genuine online subcultures before TikTok reached them — burned out within months of going viral. The algorithm surfaced the aesthetic and stripped the identity from it, compressing a lifecycle that community depth might otherwise have sustained. The line between subculture-born and viral-born trends is not fixed. Trends that begin in communities are increasingly vulnerable to the same acceleration that consumes trends with no community at all.

On the research question: the data supports the hypothesis, but with an important qualification. Subculture origin still correlates with longer, more stable lifecycles — the evidence across all four charts is consistent. But that advantage is not a guarantee, and it appears to be weakening. The data suggests the algorithm can reach into subcultural aesthetics and accelerate their lifecycle before community depth has time to protect them. The answer to the question — does subculture still give trends their staying power? — is: yes, for now, and less so than it used to.

Honest limits: eight keywords is not a representative sample. Google Trends measures search behavior, not purchase intent. Decline dates are proxied from press and search drop-off. The dataset is US and English-language dominant. pytrends instability — as an unofficial scraper, pytrends is subject to Google's rate limiting, causing timeouts and inconsistent data pulls across sessions.

Future directions: expand to 50+ trends with systematic sampling; add celebrity-driven as a third origin type; build a predictive model using early signal shape; integrate Lyst Index commercial data to connect search behavior to purchasing.

Methodology
Search interest data collected via the Google Trends Python API in Google Colab. One primary dataset: google_trends.csv (weekly search interest, 8 keywords, 261 weeks, May 2021 – May 2026, 0–100 relative index normalized to each keyword's own peak). Lifecycle classifications, peak weeks, and historical averages were derived from this dataset. Visualizations built in HTML/CSS/JavaScript. Heatmap: luminance encoding on a single greyscale channel, accurate for ordered quantitative data without hue confounds (Ware, 2004). Slope chart: two-timepoint change with collision-offset labels and connector lines to preserve data accuracy (Tufte, 1983). Sparkline small multiples: direct lifecycle shape encoding allows visual comparison across all eight trends simultaneously without requiring derived statistics (Cairo, 2013). Dumbbell chart: two-endpoint connected dot plot for before/after comparison, where line length encodes magnitude of change. Accent color #D4512A applied only on interaction to preserve editorial greyscale at rest. Progressive disclosure structure reduces cognitive load across sections (Cairo, 2013). Position encoding used throughout as the most accurate perceptual channel for ordered data (Cleveland & McGill, 1984).
